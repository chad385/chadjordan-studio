The story

The client needed to shoot outside the US. The question was where.

We were prepping for a shoot. The date was fast approaching, and the client was racking up costs because they knew they had to go international for budget reasons — but couldn't decide between Canada, South Africa, and Portugal. We ended up doing location scouting for all three. A decision finally came down four weeks before the shoot started.

That's not a horror story. That's Tuesday. That's what international production looks like when the stakes are real and the variables are infinite — flights, permits, crew rates, weather windows, exchange rates, time zones, and a client who needed to be certain before they could commit.

I've been in that room more times than I can count. And I don't tell that story to criticize anyone in it. I tell it because it's the clearest illustration I know of what production actually costs — not in dollars, but in exposure.

The career

What the grind teaches you isn't style. It's judgment.

Twenty-plus years of real creative direction across music retail, healthcare, consumer goods, defense tech, and food and beverage. Guitar Center. Sprouts. Inizio Evoke. A pickle company that got on Good Morning America. Two Webby honorees. Agency pitches where the AI animations I built were the reason we were in the room.

When to push the brief and when to serve it. When the location is the idea and when it's just expensive. When the talent is wrong and the client hasn't seen it yet. When the budget is a creative constraint and when it's just a wall.

That judgment doesn't come from a course. It comes from being wrong in front of clients and right in front of deadlines, enough times, for long enough, that the pattern becomes instinct.

The moment

Same creative standards.
Different set of variables.

When AI image generation got serious, I didn't see a threat. I saw a location that had solved the variable problem.

Not because it's cheaper — though it is. Not because it's faster — though it is. Because for the first time, a creative director with real directorial instincts could control the entire production environment without committing to it first. You could scout all three locations simultaneously. You could recast without rebooking. You could change the weather.

Real productions will always exist. Crews of twenty, permits pulled six weeks out, flights booked. And sometimes you shoot internationally because the dollar goes three times further and the light is better anyway. The brief didn't change. The variables did.

THIRD is the same logic applied to the shoot itself. No passport required.

The question was never real vs. AI. It was always: what does this job actually require?
